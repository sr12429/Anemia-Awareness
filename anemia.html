What Is Anemia?

Anemia is a condition in which the body does not have enough healthy red blood cells or hemoglobin to carry oxygen throughout the body. Hemoglobin is the protein in red blood cells responsible for transporting oxygen from the lungs to tissues and organs. Without enough oxygen, the body cannot function as efficiently, causing fatigue, weakness, and other symptoms.

Understanding Anemia

Anemia is one of the most common blood disorders and can affect people of all ages. Iron-deficiency anemia is the most common type, especially among adolescent girls and women because of increased iron needs during growth and blood loss during menstruation. Depending on the cause, anemia may develop suddenly or gradually and range from mild to severe.

Common Symptoms

  • Fatigue or unusual tiredness
  • Weakness
  • Pale or yellowish skin
  • Shortness of breath
  • Dizziness or headaches
  • Cold hands and feet
  • Rapid or irregular heartbeat

What Causes Anemia?

Anemia occurs when the body does not make enough healthy red blood cells, loses too many red blood cells, or destroys them faster than they can be replaced. Common causes include iron deficiency, vitamin B12 or folate deficiency, heavy menstrual bleeding, chronic diseases, inherited blood disorders such as sickle cell disease, and certain medical conditions that affect red blood cell production.

Who Is at Risk?

Anyone can develop anemia, but some people are at greater risk. These include teenage girls and women with heavy menstrual periods, pregnant individuals, people with poor dietary iron intake, individuals with certain chronic diseases, older adults, and people with inherited blood disorders.

Prevention Tips

Some forms of anemia can be prevented by eating a balanced diet rich in iron, vitamin B12, and folate. Foods such as lean meats, beans, lentils, spinach, fortified cereals, and dark leafy greens are excellent sources of iron. Eating vitamin C-rich foods, such as oranges, strawberries, or bell peppers, alongside iron-rich foods helps the body absorb iron more effectively.

When Should You See a Doctor?

If you experience persistent fatigue, weakness, dizziness, shortness of breath, or other symptoms of anemia, talk with a healthcare provider. Blood tests, including a Complete Blood Count (CBC), can help diagnose anemia and determine its cause so the appropriate treatment can begin.
